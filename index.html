ACCESSING MENTAL HEALTH

A data investigation about mental health access in UK universities

Story by Nuria Riquelme-Palazon

Making visible mental health among students

The number of students seeking for counselling has increased in all universities in the UK in the past five years, a trend that doesn't seem to stop.

Currently, the NHS is registering a two week to get a first appointment regarding Mental Health problems that, at the same time, it could have lead to university students looking for counselling in their universities more than before

A report from the West Middlands Mental Health Comission 2016 highlighted that in those cases where young people are sufering from mental health problems "intervent is a priority, in order to reduce other mental health issues in adulthood.

The stigma around this health matter has reduced in the past years in the UK thank to projects like Time To Change but it is, still, a problem that needs to be adress as a priority, above all among young adults and teenagers, one of the most vulnerable groups.

In this context is where Accessing Mental Health born.

How many students are actually seeking for counselling in their universities? And how many staff hae the university to cover their need? And is the waiting list any shorter than in the NHS?

Accessing Mental Health has look into this numbers. After sending Freedom of Information (FOI) to all UK universities, and getting 61 responses it's beeing possible to draw rough idea of the sittuation of students seeking for counseling in their universities, even if not every university had all the information requested.

The questions wanted to look into the number of students seeking for counselling , the number of staff and serices avaliable for this matter, the budget to cover those needs and the average on waiting list to get a first appointment.

Firstly, accessing mental health has found out that 59 from the 61 universities that responded have increase the number of students seeking for counselling or mental health advice from the period 2011/2012, an average growth of 60% in the UK, being Cardiff University the one that has experimented the highest increase with 161.43% more students than in 2015/2016.

The below figure shows how has changed the number of students in the past five years, where you can drag the mouse to check each university and who has evolved.

These numbers don't mean, necesary, that exists an increment in the number of young people suffering from mental health problems.

Jim Chapman, head of the Mental Health department at Birmingham City University, has declared for this project that " this trend doesn't mean necesary that there is more young people or students with mental health issues, but that there is less stigma when it comes to ask for help".

MORE STUDENTS SEEKING FOR COUNSELLING COULD MEAN LESS STIGMA AROUND MENTAL HEALTH PROBLEMS

A positive finding in this investigation (that you will appreciate in the following chart) is that almost all universities that responded to the FOI have experimented and increment in their budget from the period 2011/2012, being Essex University the one that has invested more

On the other hand, the average of waiting list to get a first appointment with one of the members of the counselling team is 13.09 days, just slighly lower than the waiting list in the NHS. In the graph bellow you will appreciate that King's College London is the university with the longest waiting list, 46 days, and the University of Manchester has the shortest one, just one day.

Ultimately, the number of staff and services allocated for conselling it has varied in the past five years in diferent ways in all universities, relying or not in the number of student and the budget for he university.

From the 61 universities analysed, 29 have increased the number of staff in the past five years, while the number of students support services for this matter have continue the same in most of the universities from 2011/2012.

All the information exposed is being analysed and condensed in a single Excel spreadsheet, avaliable for further clarification here.

Some views about mental health in the UK universities

Trying to back up this investigation and give answer to some of the results previously reflected, three mental health experts were contact for this project: Jim Chapman, head of the mental mental health deppartment at Birmingham City University ;Sarah Appleby mental health program lead from the West Midlands Academic Health Science Network and Mohammed Mumit, mental health awarness coordinator .
