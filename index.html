ACCESSING MENTAL HEALTH

A data investigation about mental health access in UK universities

Story by Nuria Riquelme-Palazon

Making mental health visible for students

The number of students seeking for counselling support has increased in all universities in the UK in the past five years, an investigation by a member of Birmingham Eastside has found.

Currently, the NHS is registering a two week wait period to get a first appointment regarding mental health problems that, at the same time, could have lead to university students to look for counselling in their universities more than before

A report from the West Middlands Mental Health Comission 2016 highlighted that in those cases where young people are sufering from mental health problems, "intervent is a priority, in order to reduce other mental health issues in adulthood.

In the past years, the stigma around this health matter has reduced in the UK thank to projects like Time To Change but it is still a problem that needs to be adressed as a health care priority, above all among young adults and teenagers, one of the most vulnerable groups.

In this context is where Accessing Mental Health born.

After sending Freedom of Information (FOI) to all UK universities, 61 have made possible to draw rough idea of the sittuation of students seeking for counseling in their universities.

The questions wanted to look into the number of students seeking for counselling , the number of staff and serices avaliable for this matter, the budget to cover those needs and the average on waiting list to get a first appointment.

Firstly, the investigation has found out that 59 from the 61 universities that responded have increase the number of students seeking for counselling or mental health advice from the period 2011/2012, an average growth of 60%, being Cardiff University the one that has experimented the highest increase with 161.43% more students than in 2015/2016.

However, these numbers don't mean, necesary, that exists an increment in the number of young people suffering from mental health problems.

Jim Chapman, head of the Mental Health department at Birmingham City University, said to this project that " this trend doesn't mean necesary that there is more young people or students with mental health issues, but that there is less stigma when it comes to ask for help".

More students seeking for counselling could mean less stigma around mental health issues

A positive finding in this investigation (that you will appreciate in the following chart) is that almost all universities that responded to the FOI have experimented and increment in their budget from the period 2011/2012, being Essex University the one that has invested more

On the other hand, the average of waiting list to get a first appointment with one of the members of the counselling team is 13.09 days, just slighly lower than the waiting list in the NHS. In the graph bellow you will appreciate that King's College London is the university with the longest waiting list, 46 days, and the University of Manchester has the shortest one, just one day.

Ultimately, the number of staff and services allocated for conselling has varied in the past five years in diferent ways in all universities, relying on number of students, budget or other variables.

From the 61 universities analysed, 29 have increased the number of staff in the past five years, while the number of students support services for this matter hasn't varied in most of the universities from 2011/2012.

Some views about mental health in the UK universities

Jim Chapman, head of the Mental Health deppartment at Birmingham City University; Sarah Appleby Mental health Program Lead for the West Midlands Academic Health Science Network and Mohammed Mumit, Mental Health Awarness Coordinator at Birmingham City University have given their views about the current state of the counselling in universities, what is working and what should improve (click in the dots bellow to see a piece from each interviewee).

The main issue around mental health that has arose among all three is the need of training qualified staff, to be able to responde to people's needs. .

Mohammed highlighted how there are some environmental changes that need to be done, and the first step is, "having strong mental health advisors".

But which could be the first step to get more and better counsellors to help students? Sarah Appleby claimed that is important to raise awarness, not just those suffering but for everybody that could be pontecially affected by a mental health problem. "A first step could be giving teacher a first AID mental health training, not asking them to be clinicians, just a bit of knowledge to get a faster intervention.

A front line to prevent mental health problems among students is string mental health advisors services.
~ Mohammed Mumit

An early report from the Office for National Statistics reliased on July 31 2017 shows that The proportion of young people reporting symptoms of anxiety or depression increased from 18% in the period 2009 to 2010 to 21% in 2013 to 2014, so the important of early prevention to eliminate this problems should be a must to fight back mental health among young adults.
